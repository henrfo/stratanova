Overview

NeuroSHAP represents a fundamental advancement in explainable AI for neuroimaging, addressing critical limitations in how we interpret machine learning models applied to brain data. While SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) has become the gold standard for model interpretability, its application to neuroimaging data suffers from a crucial flaw: the assumption that features are independent.

In the brain, neighboring voxels, functionally connected regions, and anatomically defined areas exhibit strong spatial and functional dependencies. This thesis develops NeuroSHAP, an enhanced framework that incorporates domain-specific constraints through Markov Random Fields and graph-theoretical approaches, providing biologically plausible explanations that respect the brain's inherent structure and connectivity.

The Independence Problem

Traditional SHAP assumes feature independence, leading to explanations that can be neurobiologically implausible. For example, isolated voxels might be highlighted as important while ignoring their spatial neighbors, or functionally connected regions might receive contradictory importance scores.

NeuroSHAP addresses this by modeling spatial and functional dependencies explicitly, ensuring that explanations align with known principles of brain organization.

Theoretical Foundation

SHAP Limitations in Neuroimaging

Kernel SHAP approximates Shapley values by sampling feature coalitions, but this sampling assumes features can be combined independently. In neuroimaging:

  • Spatial correlation: Adjacent voxels share similar signal characteristics
  • Functional connectivity: Distant regions may be more related than spatial neighbors
  • Anatomical constraints: Brain atlases define meaningful functional units
  • Physiological dependencies: Neural activity propagates through connected networks

Markov Random Field Enhancement

P(x₁, x₂, ..., xₙ) = (1/Z) exp(-E(x₁, x₂, ..., xₙ))

Where the energy function E incorporates both data likelihood and spatial/connectivity priors

By modeling feature dependencies as a Markov Random Field, we ensure that explanations respect the conditional independence structure of brain data, leading to more coherent and interpretable results.

Methodological Approach

Multi-Scale Constraint Integration

NeuroSHAP operates at multiple spatial and functional scales:

  • Voxel-level: Local spatial smoothness constraints based on 3D neighborhoods
  • Region-level: Atlas-based grouping using anatomical and functional parcellations
  • Network-level: Long-range connectivity constraints derived from diffusion MRI or functional connectivity
  • Hierarchical: Multi-resolution analysis from individual voxels to large-scale networks

Graph-Theoretical Framework

The brain's connectivity structure is represented as graphs where:

  • Nodes: Voxels, regions, or networks depending on analysis scale
  • Edges: Spatial adjacency, functional correlation, or structural connectivity
  • Weights: Strength of relationship derived from data or prior knowledge
  • Community structure: Hierarchical organization reflecting brain modularity

Technical Innovations

  • Constrained sampling: Modified coalition sampling that respects spatial and functional dependencies
  • Regularized optimization: Energy functions that balance explanation fidelity with biological plausibility
  • Multi-modal integration: Combining structural and functional connectivity for comprehensive constraints
  • Adaptive neighborhoods: Dynamic definition of relevant feature neighborhoods based on data characteristics
  • Validation metrics: Novel measures of explanation quality that incorporate neuroscientific knowledge

Implementation Strategy

Phase 1: Theoretical Development

  • Formalize the mathematical framework for constraint-aware SHAP
  • Develop efficient algorithms for MRF-based explanation generation
  • Design validation metrics for biological plausibility

Phase 2: Software Implementation

  • Extend existing SHAP libraries with neuroimaging-specific constraints
  • Integrate with popular neuroimaging analysis frameworks (Nilearn, FSL, SPM)
  • Optimize for computational efficiency on high-dimensional brain data

Phase 3: Empirical Validation

  • Compare NeuroSHAP explanations with traditional SHAP on benchmark datasets
  • Validate against ground truth from synthetic data with known patterns
  • Evaluate clinical utility in real diagnostic classification tasks

Applications & Impact

Clinical Applications

  • Disease diagnosis: More interpretable biomarkers for Alzheimer's, schizophrenia, and autism
  • Treatment planning: Understanding which brain regions drive therapeutic decisions
  • Precision medicine: Patient-specific explanations for personalized treatment
  • Clinical validation: Explanations that align with known neuroanatomy and pathophysiology

Research Advancement

  • Hypothesis generation: Scientifically grounded explanations that suggest new research directions
  • Method validation: Tools for assessing whether ML models learn biologically meaningful patterns
  • Cross-study reproducibility: Consistent explanations across different datasets and populations
  • Multi-modal integration: Framework for combining insights from different imaging modalities

Technical Challenges & Solutions

Computational Complexity

Adding constraint satisfaction to SHAP significantly increases computational demands. Solutions include:

  • Approximate inference: Efficient sampling strategies that maintain constraint satisfaction
  • Hierarchical decomposition: Multi-scale analysis to reduce problem dimensionality
  • GPU acceleration: Parallel computation of constraint-aware explanations
  • Sparse representations: Exploiting brain data sparsity for computational efficiency

Constraint Selection

Determining appropriate constraints for different neuroimaging contexts requires:

  • Data-driven approaches: Learning connectivity structure from the data itself
  • Prior knowledge integration: Incorporating established neuroanatomical atlases
  • Adaptive methods: Selecting constraints based on specific research questions
  • Validation frameworks: Assessing constraint quality through cross-validation

Future Extensions

The NeuroSHAP framework opens several avenues for future development:

  • Temporal dynamics: Extending to time-series neuroimaging data (fMRI, EEG, MEG)
  • Multi-modal fusion: Unified explanations across structural, functional, and molecular imaging
  • Causal inference: Moving beyond correlation to causal explanations of brain-behavior relationships
  • Real-time applications: Online explanation generation for brain-computer interfaces
  • Population studies: Explaining differences across demographic groups while preserving individual privacy

Expected Contributions

Scientific Impact

  • First comprehensive framework for biologically-constrained ML explanation in neuroimaging
  • Novel algorithms that bridge explainable AI and computational neuroscience
  • Validation of whether popular ML models learn neurobiologically meaningful patterns

Practical Impact

  • Open-source software tools for the neuroimaging community
  • Improved clinical decision support through interpretable AI
  • Enhanced reproducibility of neuroimaging machine learning studies
