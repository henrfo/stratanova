Exploring Biomarkers of Cognitive Training Responsiveness After Stroke

Status Completed
Type Bachelor Thesis
Year 2024
Institution University of Oslo
← Back to Projects
Neuroscience Stroke Machine Learning Structural MRI Blood Biomarkers

Overview

This bachelor thesis explores an innovative multimodal machine learning approach to understanding cognitive training outcomes in stroke patients. By integrating structural MRI data, blood biomarkers, and normative deviation metrics, the project investigates potential predictive relationships between brain features and responsiveness to cognitive interventions.

The research addresses a critical challenge in stroke rehabilitation: why do some patients respond better to cognitive training than others? Understanding these individual differences could lead to more personalized and effective rehabilitation strategies.

R² = 0.22 In-sample Performance
54 Stroke Patients
951 Healthy Controls
3 Data Modalities

Methods & Approach

Data Integration

The project employed a sophisticated multimodal approach, combining:

  • Structural MRI: High-resolution brain imaging to assess tissue integrity and regional volumes
  • Blood Biomarkers: Inflammatory and neurodegenerative markers that might influence recovery
  • Normative Modeling: Comparing patient data against healthy control distributions to identify individual deviations

Machine Learning Pipeline

Developed an end-to-end machine learning pipeline featuring:

  • Advanced feature engineering from multimodal neuroimaging data
  • Normative deviation metrics calculated from 951 healthy controls
  • Ensemble methods combining multiple predictive models
  • Cross-validation strategies adapted for small sample sizes

Key Findings

  • Achieved R² = 0.22 in-sample performance, suggesting modest but meaningful predictive signal
  • Identified specific brain regions whose structural integrity correlates with training response
  • Blood inflammatory markers showed surprising associations with cognitive improvement
  • Normative deviation patterns revealed patient subgroups with distinct response profiles

Technical Implementation

Technologies & Tools

  • Python: Primary programming language for analysis pipeline
  • Scikit-learn: Machine learning model development and validation
  • Nilearn: Neuroimaging data processing and visualization
  • FreeSurfer: Automated brain segmentation and parcellation
  • R: Statistical analysis and advanced visualization

Challenges & Solutions

The project navigated several technical challenges:

  • Small sample size: Implemented robust cross-validation and regularization techniques
  • High dimensionality: Used feature selection and dimensionality reduction methods
  • Missing data: Developed imputation strategies that preserve biological validity
  • Multimodal integration: Created novel normalization approaches for combining heterogeneous data types

Impact & Future Directions

This exploratory work provides a foundation for precision medicine approaches in stroke rehabilitation. While the predictive performance suggests room for improvement, the multimodal framework and identified biomarker candidates offer promising directions for future research.

Potential Applications

  • Personalized rehabilitation planning based on individual biomarker profiles
  • Early identification of patients who may benefit from intensive interventions
  • Development of targeted therapies addressing specific deficit patterns

Next Steps

Future work could expand this research by incorporating longitudinal data, functional imaging modalities, and genetic markers. Validation in larger, independent cohorts will be essential for clinical translation.

Collaborations & Acknowledgments

This project was conducted in collaboration with the Department of Psychology at the University of Oslo, with guidance from leading researchers in cognitive neuroscience and stroke rehabilitation.
